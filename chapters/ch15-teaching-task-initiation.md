# Chapter 15: Teaching Task Initiation

## Core Idea
Task initiation is the ability to begin unpleasant or tedious tasks without procrastination — it is not about tasks the child *wants* to do, but about those they have to *make themselves* do, and it can be taught through routines, visual cues, timer-based start signals, and external coaching.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Start-Time Timer Protocol (Jack's Protocol)**: A child-controlled system for initiating chores on time.
  - When to use: Child understands a chore but requires repeated nagging to begin.
  - How: (a) Child selects a start-time delay (e.g., 5 minutes for one chore, 10 for another); (b) Child uses a visual timer to track elapsed time and respond when it signals; (c) Parent provides initial cue to set the timer only; (d) If the chore isn't started within 2 minutes of the timer, child stops all other activities until the chore is done; (e) Earning a "free pass" (skip one chore) for every 5 days of on-time starts provides incentive.

- **External Coach Model (Colby's Protocol)**: A third-party mentoring system for homework initiation in older children.
  - When to use: Parent-child conflict over homework has become counterproductive, and parental "nagging" triggers resistance.
  - How: (a) Child selects a trusted coach (teacher, counselor, administrator) from the school; (b) Coach meets daily (~10 min) to plan the evening's work — what tasks, estimated time, start time, order; (c) Coach checks in 2–3 times/week by IM or phone to monitor; (d) Teachers email weekly feedback to coach on late/missing assignments; (e) Coach and child adjust goals each term; (f) Parents step back from the homework role entirely.

- **Making-a-Plan Method**: A child-ownership strategy for task initiation.
  - When to use: Child resists directives but responds well to being in control of the "when" and "how."
  - How: (a) Ask the child to decide when and how the task will get done; (b) Let the child choose the cue method (alarm clock, timer, "right after dinner"); (c) Write the plan down; (d) Child initiates and is praised for starting on time.

## Key Concepts
- **Task Initiation**: The ability to begin projects or activities without undue procrastination, in an efficient or timely manner — specifically for tasks the child finds unpleasant, aversive, or tedious.
- **"Start Times"**: A teacher- or parent-imposed time at which the child must begin a task; can be gradually transitioned from adult-imposed to child-chosen.
- **Free Pass Incentive**: A reward where the child earns the right to skip one chore/duty after a streak of on-time task initiation — leverages the power of intermittent earned relief.
- **Internalization of Routines**: The process through which repeated, cued routines become habits that the child can initiate independently — the endpoint of effective task-initiation teaching.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "timer replaces nagging" model**: a visual timer that the child sets themselves removes the parent from the role of the badgering authority — the timer becomes the neutral signal to start.
- **Think of task initiation as a "start engine"**: once the engine turns over, the child can run the task; the problem is never about doing the work, only about starting it.
- **Use the "coach instead of parent" model for older children**: when parent-child dynamics have become adversarial around homework, a neutral third party (teacher, counselor) can provide accountability without the emotional baggage.
- **Think of breaking tasks down as removing the "activation barrier"**: if a task feels overwhelming, the child won't start; each piece should feel like a "3 or less" on a 10-point difficulty scale.

## Anti-patterns
- **Nagging as a substitute for a system**: repeated reminders without a structured timer or cue system teaches the child to wait for external pressure rather than building an internal start signal.
- **Conflating task initiation with task completion**: Jack could finish his chores fine once started — the issue was purely the start; designing an intervention aimed at follow-through would miss the target.
- **Escalating to threats before creating the starting mechanism**: threatening loss of computer time may produce compliance, but it doesn't build the skill of independently initiating.
- **Assuming good intentions are enough**: Colby genuinely resolved to do better multiple times, but intention without a support system (coach, cues, plan) never produced sustained change.

## Key Takeaways
1. Separate task initiation from task completion — measure and intervene on each separately.
2. Let the child choose the start time and cue method (timer, alarm, natural event) to increase ownership and reduce resistance.
3. Use a visual timer that shows time passing (not just counting up) — the decreasing red disk of a Time Timer works well.
4. For chores, give the child control over a short delay (5 min, 10 min) before they must start — this satisfies the desire to finish "what I'm doing now."
5. For homework battles in older children, replace the parent with a third-party coach (teacher, counselor) who meets daily for 10 minutes and checks in via IM/phone.
6. When the child starts on time, praise specifically for the start, not just for the completed work.
7. If a coach is involved, ensure they get weekly teacher feedback on late/missing assignments — blind coaching is ineffective.
