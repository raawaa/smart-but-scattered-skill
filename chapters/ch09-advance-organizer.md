# Chapter 9: Advance Organizer

## Core Idea
Start with the minimum intervention necessary — simple environmental changes first, then motivation, then ready-made routines from Ch 10, and finally targeted skill work from Ch 11–21 — progressing only as far as needed for the child to succeed.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Game Plan (Intervention Hierarchy)**: A progressive decision tree for choosing interventions.
  1. **First, do the minimum** — try environmental modifications (Ch 6), scaffolding/games (Ch 7), or incentives (Ch 8) alone first.
  2. **Next, learn the principles** (this chapter) — understand the guidelines before applying any intervention.
  3. **Then tackle specific daily routines** using ready-made plans in Ch 10.
  4. **Finally, target specific executive skill weaknesses** using Ch 11–21.

- **Choosing a Routine to Tackle First**: Five criteria for prioritizing which daily routine to address.
  - Choose the routine that, if cleared up, would most improve quality of life.
  - Choose a small, easy problem for quick success and confidence building.
  - Give the child a choice of what to tackle first (increases ownership).
  - Choose one where implementation can be shared with a spouse/partner.
  - Think about long-term goals — prioritize skills critical for college and career over less important battles.

## Key Concepts
- **Minimum intervention principle**: Always try the simplest intervention first before escalating. A note on the table may be enough. Only combine approaches when necessary.
- **Goodness of fit**: What works for one person (especially the parent) may not work for the child; ask the child what will work for them.
- **Ownership**: Involving the child in designing the intervention increases buy-in and taps the drive for mastery and control.
- **Brainstorming**: The act of brainstorming solutions with the child itself builds executive skills.

## Mental Models
- Use "start small(er)" as a mantra — if you tend to overreach, take your first goal idea and cut it in half (half the time, half the work, half the challenge).
- Think of the intervention hierarchy like a ladder — start on the bottom rung and only climb when the current rung isn't enough.
- Use measurement and graphing as a feedback mechanism — graphs are incredibly reinforcing for children (and adults).
- Use "what if the child wants no part of your plans" as a troubleshooting flowchart: negotiate → more powerful reinforcers → logical consequences → outside help.

## Anti-patterns
- **Overreaching with goals**: Trying to solve everything at once. Instead, set initial goals that guarantee success (e.g., "work by yourself for 2 minutes" as a first step toward independent homework).
- **Skipping the minimum intervention**: Jumping straight to elaborate multicomponent plans when a simple note on the table would suffice.
- **Verbal reminders without visual cues**: Words "go in one ear and out the other" — always pair verbal cues with written/picture schedules, lists, or checklists.
- **Forgetting to praise when using incentives**: The goal of any incentive program is to fade tangible rewards — social reinforcers (praise) facilitate that transition.
- **Designing plans without the child**: Reduces ownership and buy-in; the plan is less likely to succeed.

## Key Takeaways
1. Always try the simplest intervention first — environmental modification, verbal scaffolding, or praise alone may be enough.
2. If you need more, start with the ready-made plans in Ch 10 before designing your own.
3. When choosing a routine, prioritize quality of life and pick one you can stick with.
4. Involve your child in designing the plan — let them own it.
5. Start with goals so small they feel almost too easy; stack successes.
6. Measure progress — count something and graph it.
7. If the child resists, negotiate first, then offer more powerful incentives, then resort to logical consequences, and finally seek outside help.
8. Practice/rehearse the procedure before implementing it in real situations.
9. Use visual reminders (lists, picture schedules, checklists) not just verbal cues.
