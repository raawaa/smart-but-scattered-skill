# Chapter 7: Teaching Executive Skills Directly

## Core Idea
There are two complementary approaches to teaching executive skills: informal methods (verbal scaffolding, games, and family activities) that build skills naturally, and formal direct interventions using a structured 6-step teaching sequence with gradual fading of supervision.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Verbal Scaffolding**: Providing explanations, guidance, and questions at the child's developmental level to support success while building understanding.
  - When to use: Everyday interactions with children of all ages, especially ages 3–8; particularly powerful when woven into routine activities (dressing, mealtime, car rides, play).
  - How: Ask rather than tell; explain rather than dictate; acknowledge feelings and their causes; encourage self-appraisal; use questions that associate objects with location, relate current activity to prior experience, describe sensory characteristics, teach cause-and-effect.

- **6-Step Direct Teaching Sequence for Executive Skills**: A systematic instructional framework for teaching any behavior.
  1. **Identify the problem behavior** — describe in specific, observable terms (not global labels like "lazy").
  2. **Set a goal** — positive restatement of the problem; involve the child; set interim goals with baseline measurement.
  3. **Outline the steps** the child needs to follow to reach the goal.
  4. **Turn the steps into a list, checklist, or short set of rules** — creates permanence, accountability, and intermediate reinforcement.
  5. **Supervise the child following the procedure** — includes practice sessions / dry runs; ongoing support without resentment.
  6. **Fade the supervision** — gradually reduce support; do not withdraw too early or too late.

- **4-Stage Fading of Supervision Hierarchy**: Progressive independence in skill execution.
  - When to use: After the teaching sequence is in place; guides how to step back systematically.
  - Stage 1: Parent directs (tells child what to do step-by-step).
  - Stage 2: Parent uses visual cues (checklist, picture schedule) saying "Look at your list."
  - Stage 3: Parent asks prompting questions ("What do you need to do?") forcing retrieval.
  - Stage 4: Child internalizes and self-directs.

## Key Concepts
- **Scaffolding**: Providing just enough support for the child to be successful, emphasizing connections between concepts, patterns, and prior knowledge.
- **Metacognition**: The complex executive skill of using thought in service of problem solving; built through extensive verbal scaffolding and explanation.
- **Baseline measurement**: Counting or timing current behavior before intervention to set realistic interim goals and track progress objectively.
- **Backward chaining**: Starting at the last step of a routine and gradually working backward; useful when the full task is overwhelming.

## Mental Models
- Use "Ask, don't tell" when you want the child to exercise thinking rather than compliance — questions build metacognition.
- Use "Explain rather than dictate" when you want the child to remember — we remember things better when we understand why.
- Think of "verbal scaffolding" as sneaking in nutrients like a fruit-and-yogurt smoothie — the child builds skills outside conflict-ridden domains.
- Use the "room cleaning" 4-stage model as a template for any routine: parent does the thinking → visual cue does the thinking → prompting question → child thinks alone.

## Anti-patterns
- **Using global labels** ("lazy," "irresponsible," "a slob"): Describes nothing specific and gives no starting point for teaching. Replace with observable behaviors.
- **Dictating without explanation** ("Because I said so!"): Undermines memory and metacognition; children need reasons to internalize.
- **Skipping practice sessions / dry runs**: Leads to failure when the real situation arises; especially critical for response inhibition and emotional control.
- **Fading supervision too early or too late**: Both mistakes undermine skill acquisition. Provide support long enough for genuine mastery, then reduce gradually.
- **Assuming one exposure is enough**: No child learns a skill in a single session; ongoing support without resentment is essential.

## Key Takeaways
1. Define the problem behavior in terms that can be seen or heard — "leaves belongings all over the living room" not "is a slob."
2. Take a baseline before intervening (count reminders, time how long tasks take) so you know if your plan is working.
3. Set interim goals that are slight improvements on the current behavior, not the final outcome right away.
4. Turn steps into a checklist — checking off items provides intermediate reinforcement and builds accountability.
5. Always conduct a practice run / dry walk-through before the real situation.
6. Fade support in defined stages from "I tell you" → "your list tells you" → "what do you need to do?" → you do it yourself.
7. Use games (board games, video games, fantasy sports) to build skills informally — transfer is more likely when you help the child see how a skill applies across situations.
