# Chapter 3: How Your Own Executive Skill Strengths and Weaknesses Matter

## Core Idea
Your own executive skill profile — whether it matches or conflicts with your child's — dramatically affects your ability to help your child; awareness of the fit (or misfit) reduces conflict and opens up new intervention strategies.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Parent-Child Executive Skill Fit Types**:
  1. **Strengths coincide with child's weaknesses**: You're strong where your child is weak — natural teaching advantage, but risk of irritation and power struggles.
  2. **Weaknesses coincide with child's weaknesses**: You share the same deficits — risk of chaos, but opportunity for mutual problem-solving and empathy.
  3. **Strengths coincide with child's strengths**: Harmony — tasks that require that skill go smoothly.
  4. **Flexibility as antidote**: Parents high in flexibility adapt more easily to any mismatch and are less likely to be irritated by their child's weaknesses.

- **Parent Executive Skills Questionnaire**: 33 items (3 per skill), 7-point Likert scale (1–7), same 11-skill structure as the child version.
  - When to use: after completing the child questionnaire; before designing interventions.
  - How: identify your three highest and three lowest scores to understand your profile.

- **Weak Organ Theory**: Under stress, executive skills that are weakest to begin with deteriorate first — like the weakest organ in an illness.
  - When to use: when you notice your patience or follow-through failing, check if your own weakest skill is being overtaxed.
  - How: use the knowledge to reduce stress, ask for help, or postpone non-essential skill-building work with your child.

- **Systematic Approach to Shared Weaknesses**: A 7-step process for when parent and child share the same weak skill.
  1. Identify child's weak skill (Ch 2 questionnaire).
  2. Identify your own weak skill (this chapter's questionnaire).
  3. Identify 2–3 recurring behaviors the child shows from that weakness.
  4. Do the same for yourself.
  5. Pick one situation where your weakness causes problems and design a strategy.
  6. Talk to your child about the shared problem and your plan.
  7. Agree on a solution for the child's problem and a cueing strategy.

## Key Concepts
- **Goodness of fit**: The degree of match between parent's and child's executive skill profiles. Poor fit leads to conflict; awareness of the fit is the first step to improving it.
- **Compensation, not blame**: Knowing your profile is not about self-criticism — it's about understanding what you bring to the dynamic so you can adjust your role.
- **Reflective listening**: Acknowledging how a problem makes the child feel ("You must feel overwhelmed") before jumping to solutions. Especially important when the child is stressed and their weakest skills are deteriorating.

## Mental Models
- **"Neat freak vs. total slob"**: When you're strong in a skill your child lacks, their behavior can feel like a personal affront. Recognizing it as a skill difference (not defiance) reduces reactivity.
- **Shared weakness as camaraderie**: "Son, you and I are both organizationally challenged — maybe we can help each other. It's the blind leading the blind, but it's all we've got!"
- **Laugh together, not weep together**: Humor about shared weaknesses preserves the relationship and opens space for mutual problem-solving.
- **Stress amplifier**: When either you or your child is under stress, the weakest skills fail first. Anticipate this and adjust expectations accordingly — a child overwhelmed by homework is not the time to enforce room cleaning.

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming the child for a skill weakness you share**: If you can't find your car keys and she can't find her shinguards, it's the same problem — not hers alone.
- **Making "constructive" suggestions during task performance**: For a child with weak emotional control or flexibility, being observed and critiqued while struggling can shut down their effort entirely.
- **Debriefing every social encounter**: Over-debriefing heightens anxiety rather than building social skills. Use judiciously and at a distance from the event.
- **Pushing through parental stress**: Periods of illness, financial strain, or marital conflict are not the time to launch new skill-building routines. Postpone and focus on maintenance.

## Key Takeaways
1. Complete the Parent Executive Skills Questionnaire honestly — ideally with input from your spouse or someone who knows you well.
2. Identify whether your profile matches or mismatches your child's for each skill domain. This awareness alone reduces conflict.
3. If you're strong where your child is weak, use your strength creatively and subtly — don't just lecture or take over.
4. If you share the same weakness, use the 7-step systematic approach. Address your own skill gap alongside your child's.
5. Under stress, your weakest skills fail first. Learn to recognize this signal about your stress level.
6. When the child is under stress, reflective listening is usually more helpful than immediate problem-solving.
7. Flexibility is the antidote to poor parent-child fit — if it's not your strength, consciously practice being adaptable.
