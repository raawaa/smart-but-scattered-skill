# Chapter 24: What's Ahead?

## Core Idea
As children enter adolescence and young adulthood, executive skill demands increase while supports decrease; parents must shift from manager to coach, using natural consequences, contingent privileges, negotiation, and the carefully calibrated experience of real-world failure to build lasting independence.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Parent-as-Coach Model**: A collaborative relationship where the parent provides a framework for problem-solving while the adolescent gathers information, generates options, and makes their own choices—the goal is not efficiency but building the child's own decision-making capacity.
  - When to use: Throughout the teenage years and transition to adulthood
  - How: Present information casually and leave it for the child to ponder → when failure comes, resist "I told you so" → offer help only if the child cannot get back on track → provide the least support necessary for them to pick themselves up

- **Natural and Logical Consequences Framework for Teens**: Consequences that flow naturally from behavior or are logically connected to it, rather than arbitrary punishments.
  - When to use: For any teenage behavior that needs correction
  - How: Natural consequence — failing to complete homework means catching up on weekends. Logical consequence — losing the chance to hang out with friends on Saturday because time is needed for studying

## Key Concepts
- **Expanded Choice Advantage**: Once children leave home, they have much greater control over what they choose to do—they can select themselves in or out of situations based on whether their executive skills are a good fit for the demands.
- **Minimum Support for Recovery**: A modification of the earlier teaching principle; instead of "minimum support necessary for success," it becomes "minimum support necessary for them to pick themselves up after a mistake and continue toward independence."
- **Communication Strategies Table**: A practical reference listing 17 negative communication patterns (name-calling, interrupting, lecturing, sarcasm, dredging up the past) and their positive replacements.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "plant a seed" model** for discussing executive skill weaknesses with teens: Present observations casually and leave them for the child to ponder, rather than lecturing. The real lesson comes from real-life experience, not your warning.
- **Use the "reality testing" model** for major decisions (starting a business, buying a car): Instead of arguing that the child's expectations are unrealistic, help them gather the actual information from real-world sources (real estate agents, banks, suppliers) so reality teaches the lesson.
- **Think of the teenage years as a "good enough" risk zone**: Teens with executive skill weaknesses often have a "good enough" mentality, and this becomes accentuated when more attractive activities compete for their time. The goal is not perfection but adequate functioning.

## Anti-patterns
- **Preventing all failure experiences**: Working too hard to protect teens from unpleasant situations, rejection, and failure leaves them unprepared for adulthood. As Henry Ford said, "Failure is only opportunity to more intelligently begin again."
- **"I told you so" after the child fails**: This closes the door to future problem-solving discussions. If you can resist this urge, a collaborative conversation is still possible.
- **Inflexible refusal to negotiate or use incentives**: Parents who refuse to make deals or who have strong misgivings about incentives deprive themselves of powerful motivators for teens.
- **Using putdowns, sarcasm, or not listening**: Nothing derails a conversation with a teenager faster than the very communication patterns you're trying to teach them not to use.
- **The "tough love, sink or swim" approach**: Pure laissez-faire is risky because failures can become too frequent or severe, leading to discouragement. Combine teaching strategies with ongoing, gradually decreasing support.

## Key Takeaways
1. Teenage challenges multiply: conformity pressure, argumentation as abstract thinking practice, independence assertion, greater demands, fewer supports, and competing interests.
2. Use natural and logical consequences rather than arbitrary punishments—they teach more effectively.
3. Make access to privileges (car, electronics) contingent on performance—this is a powerful motivator.
4. Negotiate and make deals; flexible parents have more influence than inflexible ones.
5. Adopt the communication strategies table: replace name-calling, interrupting, lecturing, sarcasm, and nagging with the positive alternatives listed.
6. Your influence persists even when feedback suggests otherwise—Peg's son acknowledged her strategies were helpful at age 25 despite his demeanor at 17.
7. Transition from parent to coach: the goal is not an efficient parent-generated solution but a framework the child can internalize through repeated practice.
8. Let real-world experience teach the lessons: having the child go to banks, calculate education expenses, and develop budgets is more effective than parental warnings.
9. Allow failure when the stakes are manageable—a ticket for an unregistered vehicle or an overdrawn account creates awareness that words cannot match.
10. The parting summary: identify and discuss strengths/weaknesses, start early, use small steps with reinforcement, point children toward resources, define what support you'll give and under what conditions, expect their end of the bargain, discuss failures openly, offer understanding when they fall, and always encourage their efforts and praise their successes.
