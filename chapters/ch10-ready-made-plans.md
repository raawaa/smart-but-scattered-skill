# Chapter 10: Ready-Made Plans for Teaching Your Child to Complete Daily Routines

## Core Idea
Twenty ready-made intervention plans for the most common daily battle zones — each following a consistent structure (list steps → create checklist → supervise → fade supervision) with built-in modifications and age adjustments — so parents can start intervening immediately without designing from scratch.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Common Structure for All 20 Routines**: Every plan contains:
  1. List of executive skills addressed (with cross-references to Ch 11–21)
  2. Age range
  3. Step-by-step teaching procedure (list steps, create checklist, rehearse, implement)
  4. Fading the supervision hierarchy (4 levels: supervise throughout → check each step → intermittent checks → end-of-task check)
  5. Modifications/adjustments (incentives, timers, picture cues for younger kids)

- **Age Adaptation Guidelines**:
  - For young children: Keep steps short, use pictures instead of words, provide side-by-side help, expect longer cueing duration.
  - For older children: Make them full partners in design, negotiate rather than dictate, use visual cues (not verbal — it sounds like nagging).

## Key Routines (Summarized by Category)

### Home Routines (1–7)
1. **Getting Ready in the Morning** (ages 7–10): Picture or written checklist of steps; child checks off each; reward if finished 15+ min early. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, working memory.
2. **Bedroom Cleaning** (ages 7–10): Step list (clothes → toys → books → desk → trash → return items); photo of "clean room" as standard. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, working memory, organization.
3. **Putting Belongings Away** (ages 7–10): List of routinely misplaced items with proper locations; pick-up time rule; penalty for forgetting. Skills: organization, task initiation, sustained attention, working memory.
4. **Completing Chores** (any age): Chore list with time estimates and schedule; timer for each step. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, working memory.
5. **Maintaining a Practice Schedule** (ages 8–14): Pre-commitment conversation about required frequency; weekly schedule; if resistance persists, reconsider whether the child actually wants to learn the skill. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, planning.
6. **Bedtime** (ages 7–10): List all pre-bed tasks; time each; calculate start time backwards from lights-out; reward = extra awake time if finished early. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, working memory.
7. **Desk Cleaning** (ages 7–10): Empty everything, label drawers, photo of clean desk as model; weekly maintenance. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, organization, planning.

### School Routines (8–14)
8. **Homework** (ages 7–14): Homework plan form — write assignments, check materials, estimate time, set start times; show parent for adjustment. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, planning, time management, metacognition.
9. **Managing Open-Ended Tasks** (ages 7–14): Two strategies — (a) make tasks more closed-ended via choices/cheat sheets/templates/rubrics, or (b) teach tolerance via think-aloud modeling. Skills: emotional control, flexibility, metacognition.
10. **Long-Term Projects** (ages 8–14): Project Planning Sheet — brainstorm topics, list resources, break into steps with timeline on a wall calendar. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, planning, time management, metacognition.
11. **Writing a Paper** (ages 8–14): 5-step process — brainstorm topics → brainstorm content → organize with Post-its → write opening paragraph ("grab the reader") → write sections with topic sentences and linking words. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, planning, organization, time management, metacognition.
12. **Studying for Tests** (ages 10–14): Distributed practice (30 min × 4 nights > 2 hours × 1 night); Menu of Study Strategies; post-test evaluation. Skills: task initiation, sustained attention, planning, time management, metacognition.
13. **Managing High-Effort Tasks** (any age): Two approaches — (a) break into very small parts with rewards per part, or (b) backward chaining (start at last step, gradually work backward through the task). Skills: task initiation, sustained attention.
14. **Organizing Notebooks/Homework** (ages 6–14): Color-coded folder system (completed vs. unfinished vs. to-file); visit office supply store together. Skills: organization, task initiation.

### Emotional/Behavioral Routines (15–20)
15. **Learning to Control Temper** (all ages): Teach a "cool-down" routine (walk away, deep breaths, count to 10); practice when calm; reward self-control.
16. **Learning to Control Impulsive Behavior** (all ages): Stop-Think-Go sequence; role-play triggering situations; immediate reinforcement for self-control.
17. **Learning to Manage Anxiety** (all ages): Teach physical relaxation (breathing, progressive muscle relaxation); create a hierarchy of fear-inducing situations; gradual exposure with support.
18. **Learning to Handle Changes in Plans** (all ages): Give advance notice of changes; use visual schedules with "change" cards; practice small, low-stakes changes.
19. **Learning Not to Cry over Little Things** (all ages): Teach emotional regulation — "Big deal vs. little deal" discrimination; reward for handling small disappointments without tears.
20. **Learning to Solve Problems** (all ages): 5-step problem-solving sequence — (1) What is the problem? (2) What are some solutions? (3) What are the pros and cons? (4) Pick one and try it. (5) Did it work?

## Key Concepts
- **Backward chaining**: Start the child at the very last step of a task, let them complete only that step, earn reward; gradually back up one step at a time. Effective for high-effort tasks and cleaning.
- **Distributed practice**: Breaking study time into smaller segments across multiple days is more effective than massed practice the night before; learning consolidates through sleep.
- **Think-aloud procedure**: Parent models the internal thought process for tackling open-ended tasks; support fades gradually over months or years for children with flexibility problems.
- **Scoring rubrics**: Provide clear criteria for assignments; essential for children who struggle with open-ended tasks.

## Mental Models
- Use the same fading hierarchy across all 20 routines: supervise throughout → check each step → intermittent checks → end-of-task check only.
- Think of backward chaining as "start at the finish line" — the child experiences success immediately at the easiest part and works backward to harder parts.
- Use the "photo of clean room" as an objective standard — eliminates arguments about quality.
- For open-ended tasks, think of the parent as a "cognitive prosthesis" — you provide the structure the child's brain struggles to generate, then gradually withdraw it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Skipping the pre-commitment conversation** (routine 5): Before signing up for lessons/sports, discuss required practice frequency and duration; if child isn't driven, parents may be forcing the skill.
- **Jumping to rewards without instruction**: If a child doesn't know HOW to do something, motivation alone won't work — teach the skill first, then motivate.
- **Using only verbal reminders**: Visual cues (checklists, picture schedules, timers) are more effective and less likely to sound like nagging.
- **Expecting independence too soon**: Most routines will need the parent as "frontal lobe" for weeks or months before fading begins.
- **Rescuing the child**: When a natural consequence has been agreed upon (e.g., coach's consequence for missing equipment), rescuing undermines the plan.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start with the routine that causes the most daily misery — improved quality of life is the bottom line.
2. Every routine follows the same formula: define the steps → make a checklist → rehearse → supervise → fade.
3. For young children: pictures, not words; shorter steps; side-by-side help assumed.
4. For adolescents: full partnership in design; negotiate; visual cues only (verbal = nagging).
5. Use timers and photos as objective, non-confrontational tools.
6. If a child resists, reconsider effort-to-reward balance — you may need more powerful incentives.
7. Backward chaining is the secret weapon for high-effort tasks and cleaning.
8. Coordinate with teachers for school-based routines — modifications require their buy-in.
9. Each routine cross-references targeted skill chapters (11–21) for deeper work.
