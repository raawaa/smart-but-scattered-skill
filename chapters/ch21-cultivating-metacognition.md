# Chapter 21: Cultivating Metacognition

## Core Idea
Metacognition is the ability to take a bird's-eye view of oneself in a situation—to self-monitor, self-evaluate, and adjust based on that evaluation; it spans both task performance (checking one's work against a standard) and social situations (reading others' reactions and adjusting one's behavior).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Metacognition (Executive Skill)**: The capacity to observe how one problem-solves, to self-monitor ("How am I doing?"), and to self-evaluate ("How did I do?"), then use that information to make better decisions going forward.
  - When to use: After completing any task (homework, chore, work assignment) or during/after any social interaction
  - How: Task domain → specific praise for thoroughness → child self-evaluates → identify "what finished looks like" → teach a self-questioning routine. Social domain → read facial expressions → interpret tone of voice → detect hidden feelings → identify how one's actions make others feel

## Key Concepts
- **Self-Questioning Routine**: A set of four questions children can internalize for any problem situation: (1) "What is the problem I need to solve?" (2) "What is my plan?" (3) "Am I following my plan?" (4) "How did I do?"
- **Rubric-Based Self-Monitoring**: Using an explicit checklist or rubric (provided by teacher/parent) that the child reviews before starting a task and then uses to evaluate their own performance afterward.
- **55-38-7 Communication Rule**: 55% of communication is facial expression, 38% is tone of voice, and only 7% is the actual words spoken—teach children to attend to the non-verbal channels.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "start-to-finish list" model** when a child's work quality is inconsistent: have the child create their own step-by-step list for what a thorough job looks like, then evaluate against it (e.g., Cory's vacuuming checklist).
- **Use the "last to speak" model** for children who dominate conversations (social metacognition): the child agrees to be the last person to speak at meals, and when they do speak, they ask for more information or compliment the speaker.
- **Think of metacognition as a "director's commentary"** on the child's own mental movie—the skill is to step outside the experience and watch oneself performing, then make adjustments.

## Anti-patterns
- **Criticism when giving feedback**: Judgment "muddies the water"—always start with a positive observation before offering specific suggestions for improvement.
- **Vague directions** ("be more careful," "pay attention," "spend more time studying"): These doom the intervention because the child has no concrete action to take. Use specific language ("check all six rows between the cars for shopping carts").
- **Tackling too many behaviors at once**: Limit the intervention to one or two behaviors in one domain (home or school, not both).
- **Expecting the child to care about the same standards you do**: What's important to you is not always important to your child—meet in the middle and settle for "good enough" rather than perfection.

## Key Takeaways
1. For task performance: provide specific praise for key elements, ask the child to self-evaluate after finishing, have them define "what finished looks like," and teach the four-question self-monitoring routine.
2. For social situations: play facial-expression guessing games (TV with sound off), label and practice identifying different tones of voice, turn emotion detection into a detective game, and ask the child how their actions might make someone feel.
3. For the Yoshi case (11yo know-it-all): have the child be the last to speak at meals, use a parent cueing system for lecturing/correcting, model compliments and questions, and let the child share their plan with the teacher.
4. For the Cory case (14yo inconsistent quality): have the child create task-specific "start-to-finish" lists, involve teachers/managers in providing rubrics, review expectations before starting, and give feedback on how well the standard was met.
5. Don't try to tackle too many behaviors at once—limit to one or two in a single domain.
6. Pair feedback with very specific behavioral direction; vague instructions guarantee failure even with a motivated child.
7. If you cannot be present to monitor, have the child self-report situations where they successfully used the skill, or arrange a cueing system with a trusted peer.
