# Chapter 19: Encouraging Flexibility

## Core Idea
Flexibility is the ability to revise plans in the face of obstacles, setbacks, new information, or mistakes; children who lack it become "thrown for a loop" when circumstances change unexpectedly, and parents must systematically desensitize them to novelty and change through graduated support and advance preparation.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Flexibility (Executive Skill)**: The capacity to adapt to changing conditions, revise plans when circumstances shift, and make the emotional adjustment necessary when facing disappointment or frustration.
  - When to use: Any situation where plans change unexpectedly, transitions occur, or the child faces novel circumstances
  - How: Reduce novelty → keep routines → provide advance warning → give scripts → reduce task complexity → offer choices → fade support gradually

## Key Concepts
- **Social Stories**: Brief vignettes with three sentence types (descriptive = key elements of situation; perspective = reactions/feelings of others; directive = strategies the child can use) that help children understand social information and handle interactions more successfully.
- **Graduated Support Fading**: Providing maximum initial support so the child never feels alone, then melting into the background as confidence grows, always offering the minimum support necessary for the child to feel successful.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "advance warning + preview" model** when the child must face a schedule change: tell them the night before, review in the morning, and bring a visual schedule to the event.
- **Use the "supported introduction" model** when the child faces a new anxiety-provoking situation: go in with them, stay until comfortable, then gradually pull back and eventually leave (like staying at a birthday party until the child settles in).
- **Think of inflexibility as a "fixed plan" problem**: the child locks onto one expected outcome; any deviation feels like a violation, not a variation.

## Anti-patterns
- **Surprising the child with last-minute changes**: This triggers meltdowns because inflexible children cannot adjust without preparation—always warn in advance.
- **Assuming the child will "get over it" with exposure alone**: Simply forcing the child through change without supports (scripts, choices, graduated fading) reinforces the fear response.
- **Giving too many choices or overly complex options**: This overwhelms the inflexible child; keep choices limited and ones you can live with.
- **Being inconsistent with the intervention plan itself**: Once a flexibility system becomes routine, the child may tantrum if YOU fail to use it—the intervention can become another fixed expectation.

## Key Takeaways
1. Environmental modifications come first: reduce novelty, keep routines, give advance warning, provide scripts, reduce task complexity, offer limited choices.
2. Walk the child through anxiety-producing situations with maximum support initially, then fade gradually—never leave them feeling "on their own."
3. Use social stories (descriptive + perspective + directive sentences) for predictably inflexible situations.
4. Give the child a default strategy for when inflexibility hits: count to 10, walk away to cool off, or ask a specific person to intervene.
5. For teens like Anna (14yo): structure a pre-approved activity list, require permission for anything outside it, use a cell phone as a communication tool, and hold family reviews twice a week.
6. For younger children like Manuel (5yo): use visual picture schedules with Velcro, preview the plan the night before and morning of, and gradually introduce both planned and unheralded changes.
7. Once the child can handle planned changes, introduce last-minute changes starting with ones the child will like (e.g., ice cream), then broaden.
