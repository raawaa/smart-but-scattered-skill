# Chapter 2: Identifying Your Child's Strengths and Weaknesses

## Core Idea
You can pinpoint your child's specific executive skill strengths and weaknesses using age-appropriate rating scales and developmental task tables, then use that profile to capitalize on strengths and systematically address weaknesses.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Executive Skills Questionnaires (Four Age Versions)**: Standardized rating scales to assess each of the 11 executive skills across 3 items per skill (33 items total, 5-point Likert scale).
  - **Preschool/Kindergarten** (ages 4–5)
  - **Lower Elementary** (Grades 1–3, ages 6–8)
  - **Upper Elementary** (Grades 4–5, ages 9–10)
  - **Middle School** (Grades 6–8, ages 11–14)
  - How: Rate each item 1–5, sum scores per 3-item skill group. The three highest scores = strengths; three lowest = weaknesses.
  - When to use: as a diagnostic starting point before designing any intervention.

- **Developmental Tasks Table (p. 30)**: Lists typical executive-skill-dependent tasks by age group (Preschool, K–2, Grades 3–5, Grades 6–8).
  - When to use: to check whether your expectations are developmentally appropriate.
  - How: compare your child's performance against the normative tasks for their age.

- **Common Executive Skill Clusters** (recurring patterns of co-occurring strengths and weaknesses):
  - **Cluster 1**: Weak Response Inhibition + Weak Emotional Control (acts/emotes without thinking)
  - **Cluster 2**: Weak Flexibility + Weak Emotional Control (meltdowns over change)
  - **Cluster 3**: Weak Task Initiation + Weak Sustained Attention + Weak Goal-Directed Persistence (slow to start, quick to quit)
  - **Cluster 4**: Weak Time Management + Weak Planning/Prioritization (doesn't know where or when to begin)
  - **Cluster 5**: Weak Working Memory + Weak Organization (loses everything, can't find anything)

## Key Concepts
- **Capitalizing on Strengths**: Use a child's strongest executive skills (e.g., goal-directed persistence) to compensate for weaker ones (e.g., task initiation). Praise strength areas specifically and frequently.
- **Addressing Weaknesses**: The three lowest scores are your intervention targets. Part III of the book provides skill-specific strategies.
- **Incidental learning vs. direct instruction**: Some children pick up executive skills through observation and loose structure; others need explicit teaching — especially in today's complex environment.
- **The School as a Window**: How teachers structure the day reveals what executive skills are expected at each grade level — observe schools to calibrate your expectations.

## Mental Models
- **Teacher as comparison group**: Teachers see 20–30 children of the same age daily. If you're unsure whether your child's behavior is typical, the teacher is your best source of objective feedback.
- **You are your child's executive skills tutor**: The parent-child ratio is more favorable than the teacher-student ratio. Use it — think of yourself as providing individualized executive skill coaching.
- **Patterns over individual scores**: Executive skill weaknesses tend to cluster. Addressing one skill in a cluster (e.g., flexibility) often helps another (e.g., emotional control).
- **Strengths compensate for weaknesses**: A child with weak task initiation but strong goal-directed persistence can be motivated with a "points-to-reward" system. A child with weak organization but strong working memory can find things even in a messy room.

## Anti-patterns
- **Comparing only to same-age peers without considering the range of normal**: Development varies widely. Some 5-year-olds remember to brush teeth on their own; many others do not at age 8.
- **Pulling back supervision at middle school just because the child wants independence**: Brain growth at age 11–12 is uneven and unpredictable. More support, not less, is critical during this period, like removing training wheels from a bike.
- **Expecting consistency**: The difference between "can do a task" and "can do it consistently every day" is enormous. Inconsistency is itself a sign of a skill weakness.
- **Assuming school performance equals home performance**: Home is less structured, has more stressors (siblings), and may have different expectations. A child can succeed at school and still need help at home.

## Key Takeaways
1. Use the age-appropriate Executive Skills Questionnaire to create a profile of your child's strengths and weaknesses.
2. The three highest-scoring skills are your leverage points — praise them specifically, use them to compensate for weaknesses.
3. The three lowest-scoring skills are your intervention targets. Address one skill in a cluster to help the others.
4. Cross-reference your findings against the Developmental Tasks Table to ensure your expectations are age-appropriate.
5. Talk to your child's teacher — they have a built-in norm group and can offer objective perspective.
6. Praise any use of executive skills, not just in areas of strength. This may be the most underused strategy parents have.
7. Read all of Part II before jumping to Part III interventions — the foundation chapters make your interventions more effective.
