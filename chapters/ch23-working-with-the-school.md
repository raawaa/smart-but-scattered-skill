# Chapter 23: Working with the School

## Core Idea
For genuine improvement in children with executive skill weaknesses, everybody has to work harder—teachers, parents, and the child—and the most effective approach is nonadversarial collaboration where you lead with "here's what we can do" and follow with "what do you think would help?"

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Everybody-Has-to-Work-Harder Principle**: Teachers must do more than for typical students, parents must provide more supervision and monitoring, and the child must put in more effort; tensions arise when any one party is not pulling their weight.
  - When to use: Any school-based executive skill intervention
  - How: Start the conversation with what you're willing to do → follow with an open-ended question about what the teacher thinks would help → use specific examples of the problem → offer concrete support (weekly emails, coming in to clean out the desk)

- **RTI (Response to Intervention) Model** for executive skills: A tiered assessment-intervention approach that begins with high-quality classroom instruction, then adds increasingly intensive interventions as needed, without requiring a formal disability label.
  - When to use: When the school has RTI options available and the child needs targeted support before considering special education
  - How: Identify the specific problem → design intervention with all parties agreeing to work harder → collect data on whether it works → escalate only if it doesn't

- **Measurable IEP Goals for Executive Skills**: Annual goals with precise measurement procedures for each executive skill domain.
  - When to use: When the child qualifies for special education and executive skill deficits are documented
  - How: State the goal in measurable terms → define how, when, and by whom progress is measured → use objective data (counts, percentages, rating scales, natural data)

## Key Concepts
- **Section 504 Plan**: A civil-rights-based accommodation plan that ensures children with disabilities have equal access to education (extended time, modified tests, breaks, modified grading). Does not require specialized instruction.
- **Special Education**: Requires an educational disability that adversely affects performance and necessitates specialized instruction. Can address organization, time management, and planning through direct teaching.
- **Sample IEP Goals Table** (pp. 294-296): Includes measurable annual goals for response inhibition, working memory, emotional control, sustained attention, task initiation, planning/prioritization, organization, time management, goal-directed persistence, and flexibility—each with specific measurement procedures.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "nonadversarial lead" model** with teachers: Say "Here's what we can do, and here's what we're prepared to ask our child to do" before making any requests. Teachers are more willing to work harder when parents lead by example.
- **Use the "weekly email" model** for follow-through: Email teachers weekly to ask about missing assignments rather than expecting them to send a report. Replying to an email is easier than generating one, reducing the teacher's burden.
- **Think of 504 Plans as accommodations and Special Education as instruction**: 504 = modify the environment; Special Ed = teach the skill directly.

## Anti-patterns
- **Blame or accusations when approaching teachers**: An adversarial approach is counterproductive; start from shared concern for the child.
- **Letting the child be the communication channel between home and school**: Children are notoriously poor informants—establish direct parent-teacher communication instead.
- **Assuming teachers will remember accommodations without support**: If the teacher has executive skill weaknesses too, offer help ("Is there something I could do to help?") rather than complaining about inconsistency.
- **Writing vague IEP goals** ("student will improve organization"): Goals must be precise and measurable with defined data-collection procedures.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start every school conversation with what you're willing to do, then ask the teacher for their ideas—nonadversarial collaboration works best.
2. If a teacher suggests medication, respond by acknowledging your concerns and describing what non-medical interventions you'd like to try first.
3. If the teacher agrees to accommodations but forgets, offer help (weekly email reminders, coming to school to help clean out the desk) rather than complaining.
4. The most effective teachers for executive skills create whole-class routines and embed skill instruction in subject matter (breaking down long-term assignments, homework routines, end-of-day check routines).
5. Executive skill problems warrant services when they interfere with school success—failing grades or grades that don't reflect potential.
6. Use the RTI model: design a specific intervention with the teacher, collect data, and only escalate to formal evaluation if needed.
7. When writing IEP goals, use the sample goals table in this chapter as a template—each goal must be measurable, with defined what/when/by whom for data collection.
8. If the school doesn't follow the IEP, ask for the data to be shared on a regular schedule via email. If collaboration fails despite best efforts, consider hiring an advocate or lawyer.
