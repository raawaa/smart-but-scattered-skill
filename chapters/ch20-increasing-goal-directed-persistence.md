# Chapter 20: Increasing Goal-Directed Persistence

## Core Idea
Goal-directed persistence is the ability to set a goal and work toward it without being sidetracked by competing interests; because it is one of the last executive skills to mature, parents must systematically build stamina by starting with ultra-short, child-chosen goals with visible progress tracking and gradually extending the time horizon.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Goal-Directed Persistence (Executive Skill)**: Setting a goal and sustaining effort toward it over time despite competing interests, frustration, or lack of immediate payoff.
  - When to use: Any task requiring sustained effort (chores, savings, practice, long-term school assignments)
  - How: Start brief → use the child's own interest → provide concrete progress tracking → gradually extend time → use visual reminders → ensure the reward is valued and not freely accessible

## Key Concepts
- **Progressive Goal Staircasing**: Breaking a distant goal into very small, achievable steps where each step is within sight (in time and space) and rewarded, then gradually increasing the distance between steps.
- **Concrete Progress Representation**: Using visual tokens (puzzle pieces, jar tokens, thermometer charts) to make progress toward a goal tangible and measurable for children who cannot hold the end state in mind.
- **Jump-Start Strategy**: Timing the start of a savings or persistence plan to coincide with a windfall (birthday money, holiday gifts) so the child sees rapid initial progress and stays motivated.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "puzzle piece" model** for savings goals: laminate a picture of the goal, cut it into pieces, and assign each piece to a savings installment—the child literally "builds" the goal with each deposit.
- **Use the "before-preferred-task" model** for low-motivation tasks: position a non-preferred task (chores, practice) immediately before a preferred activity (screen time, play)—the preferred activity becomes the natural incentive.
- **Think of goal-directed persistence as a "time horizon" muscle**: a child's time horizon is much shorter than an adult's; the end must always be in sight for the child, so never expect them to persist toward a goal months away without intermediate milestones.

## Anti-patterns
- **Using encouragement or insistence alone** ("just try harder," "keep going"): These have no lasting impact because the child lacks the skill, not the will.
- **Setting goals the parent chooses rather than the child wants**: For initial persistence training, start with what the child already wants to work on (Lego structures before bedroom cleaning).
- **Making the reward freely accessible outside the plan**: If the child already has unlimited access to video games, offering game time as a reward for persistence will not motivate them.
- **Expecting months of saving without visible progress**: Children need to see the end—use concrete progress trackers and avoid overly ambitious time frames.
- **Nagging with verbal reminders**: Especially ineffective with adolescents; visual reminders (pictures on the wall) work far better.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start early with very brief tasks where the goal is within sight—puzzles with few pieces, simple chores contained in a small space.
2. Always begin with goals the child wants to work on; let success build motivation for less preferred tasks later.
3. Give the child something to look forward to after the chore is done; if stamina is very low, provide a reward after partial completion.
4. Use concrete progress representation: tokens in a jar, puzzle pieces, coloring sections of a drawing, or thermometer charts.
5. Place visual reminders of the goal (picture of the desired item) where the child sees them daily—on the bedroom wall or refrigerator.
6. For the Samuel case (5yo quitter): break goals into tiny steps (any contact with the ball, not home runs), use a chart to track data, and gradually increase demands.
7. For the Jared case (9yo can't save): time the plan around a birthday for a jump-start, use a laminated picture-puzzle savings tracker, and take the child to play the demo every 2 weeks to stay connected to the goal.
