# Chapter 8: Motivating Your Child to Learn and Use Executive Skills

## Core Idea
Motivation strategies — from simple praise to formal incentive systems with behavior contracts — are essential for getting children to practice and internalize executive skills, and a positive approach consistently outperforms punishment.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Effective Praise Framework**: Specific, immediate, and individualized praise is far more powerful than global praise.
  - When to use: Whenever the child displays the target behavior; aim for a 3:1 ratio of positive to corrective statements.
  - Characteristics: (1) Delivered immediately after the behavior; (2) Specifies the particulars of the accomplishment; (3) Provides information about the value of the accomplishment; (4) Acknowledges effort; (5) Orients child toward self-appreciation and problem solving.

- **Grandma's Law (First/Then)**: Less preferred tasks must precede more preferred tasks.
  - When to use: As the simplest motivational strategy for everyday routines; often sufficient on its own.
  - How: "As soon as you pick up your bedroom, you can play video games" — stress access to the desired activity, not lack of access.

- **Formal Incentive System Design**: A 4-step process for building structured motivation.
  1. Describe the problem behavior and set a specific, observable goal.
  2. Decide on rewards and contingencies — build a reward menu with daily, weekly, and long-term options; points-based system.
  3. Write a behavior contract — specifying what the child agrees to do, what parents will do, points/rewards, and consequences for failure.
  4. Evaluate and make changes — expect to tinker; rare that it works perfectly the first time.

## Key Concepts
- **Kindling a positive drive state**: Having something to look forward to after an aversive task energizes effort and counters negative feelings; even small rewards work.
- **Positive wording of incentives**: "As soon as you finish X, you can do Y" is more effective than "You can't do Y until you finish X" — keeps the child's eyes on the prize.
- **Behavior contract**: A written agreement that defines the child's responsibilities, parents' supports, points/rewards system, and consequences; loophole-proofing is expected.
- **Home–school report card**: A coordination tool where teachers communicate points earned at school; rewards delivered at home, leveraging parents' wider array of reinforcers.

## Mental Models
- Use praise as the "first-line" motivator — it is the most underappreciated tool for behavior change parents have.
- Use "something fun at the finish line" whenever the task is aversive — even adults reward themselves with games or treats after difficult work.
- Think of the reward menu as a contract negotiation — the bigger the task effort, the more powerful the reward needs to be.
- Use the home–school report card as a bridge — positive communication between school and home around a shared goal.

## Anti-patterns
- **Punishment-only approach**: Tells children what NOT to do, not what to do; damages the parent-child relationship; leads to "I've got nothing left to lose."
- **Global/nonspecific praise** ("Good girl!" "Great job!"): Far less effective than praise that names the specific behavior and its value.
- **Negative wording** ("You can't play video games until…"): Increases task refusal and power struggles compared to positive framing.
- **Stingy rewards**: Parents often underestimate the effort required of children with executive skill deficits; if the task looks bigger than the reward, the child resists.
- **Rigid contracts**: Failing to adjust points, rules, or rewards after the first attempt; tinkering is normal and expected.
- **Neglecting siblings**: A formal system for one child can breed resentment unless explained, and siblings can have their own (even informal) goals.

## Key Takeaways
1. Aim for a 3:1 ratio of positive to corrective statements — it's hard but worth working toward.
2. Use specific, immediate praise that names the behavior and its positive impact.
3. Frame incentives positively: "As soon as you finish your homework, you can play" — not "No video games until homework is done."
4. When simple praise isn't enough, design a formal system: points from a reward menu, with daily and long-term options.
5. Write a behavior contract that both parent and child sign; expect to revise it.
6. For school problems, link school performance with home rewards via a home–school report card.
7. If the child resists all engagement, fall back on logical/natural consequences: privileges route through you.
8. If problems are severe enough, seek outside help (therapist, coach, tutor) — see Ch 22.
