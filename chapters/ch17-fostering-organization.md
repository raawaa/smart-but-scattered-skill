# Chapter 17: Fostering Organization

## Core Idea
Organization is the ability to establish and maintain a system for arranging or keeping track of important items — it is built by providing the child with structures, supervising daily use of those structures, and gradually fading support, starting with the smallest, highest-priority domain first.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Two-Part Organizational Teaching Method**: The fundamental approach to building any organizational habit.
  - When to use: Any domain where the child cannot maintain order (backpack, desk, room, belongings).
  - How: (a) Put a system in place (bins, shelves, folders, labels, checklists); (b) Supervise daily use of the system — initially on-site, then with a cue to start and a check-in when finished; (c) Involve the child in designing the system to increase buy-in; (d) Photograph the "ideal state" as a visual reference; (e) Work on one domain at a time until it is reliable.

- **Family Cleanup Accountability System (Rose Family Protocol)**: A group-based incentive system for multiple children.
  - When to use: Multiple children leave belongings strewn around common areas.
  - How: (a) A jar holds $25 in quarters at the start of each week; (b) All out-of-place belongings must be picked up by 5:00 PM daily; (c) Each item left out costs one quarter (removed from jar); (d) Impounded items stay in a box for 24 hours; (e) Needed items (homework, sports gear) can be "bought back" with allowance; (f) Remaining money at week's end is spent on a group activity; (g) A whiteboard with each child's name serves as a daily checklist; (h) An alarm clock (set for 4:30 PM) cues the start.

- **Self-Designed Organizational System (Devon's Protocol)**: A child-driven approach for older children with broader organizational deficits.
  - When to use: The child has failed at system-imposed organizational schemes and feels discouraged; natural consequences ("let him suffer") have not worked.
  - How: (a) Let the child take inventory of what they own and decide categories; (b) Let the child choose storage supplies and labeling method (children may resist visible labels — use removable Velcro labels); (c) Agree on a cleaning checklist and photograph the ideal arrangement; (d) Use a computer reminder cue (e.g., daily pop-up); (e) Parents check in every other day before screens are allowed; (f) For homework, a homeroom teacher checks morning (homework brought) and afternoon (assignments recorded + materials packed) using a checklist.

## Key Concepts
- **Organization**: The ability to establish and maintain a system for arranging or keeping track of important items — distinct from cleanliness or tidiness as a preference.
- **Photo as Visual Standard**: A photograph of the organized space (desk, bedroom) serves as an objective, non-negotiable reference point for what "organized enough" looks like — useful when parent and child differ on standards.
- **Organized-Enough Standard**: The minimal level of organization that allows the child to function (e.g., find homework, keep sports equipment, not lose valuables) — not necessarily the parent's ideal of neatness.
- **Impound-and-Redeem System**: Confiscating out-of-place items for a defined period; the child can buy back essential items with allowance — combines natural consequence with a path to reclaim.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "start very small" model**: if organization is a broad problem, pick one domain (e.g., the homework folder or a single desk drawer) and only expand after that is reliable for at least a month.
- **Think of the child's obliviousness as a "blindness" not defiance**: many disorganized children literally do not see the mess; a photo of the "organized" state gives them a concrete target rather than relying on their perception.
- **Use the "organized enough" framing**: set an achievable standard with the child, not the parent's ideal — this prevents discouragement and makes success feel attainable.
- **Think of labeling as a "search system"**: labels (even removable ones) convert a pile of items into addressable categories, reducing the cognitive load of finding and returning items.

## Anti-patterns
- **Trying to fix every organizational domain at once**: tackling homework, room, sports equipment, and backpack simultaneously is unsustainable for child and parent; pick one and stabilize first.
- **Cleaning the child's room FOR them**: this removes the natural feedback loop and teaches helplessness; the parent should work *with* the child, breaking the task down and supervising.
- **Imposing a parent's standard of neatness**: the organizationally gifted parent sees mess as intolerable; the child may not even notice it. The photo-as-standard method bridges this gap with an agreed objective target.
- **Natural consequences without skill-building**: Devon's parents initially "let him suffer" (lost iPod, failing grades, coaches upset) — the suffering didn't teach him to organize; it only discouraged him.
- **Designing a system without child involvement**: systems imposed by parents are rarely maintained; children need to own the categories, storage choices, and labeling method.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start with ONE organizational domain — the highest-priority one (usually homework) — and stabilize it before moving on.
2. Involve the child in designing the system: let them choose bins, labels, and the order of the cleaning routine.
3. Photograph the "ideal organized state" as a visual reference the child can check against.
4. Use removable labels (Velcro, sticky notes) if the child objects to visible labels in their personal space.
5. For multiple children, a shared incentive system with group reward (money jar, group activity) creates peer accountability.
6. For the organizationally challenged parent, simplify the cleanup system to just before-bed pickup with impounded items redeemable with allowance.
7. Teachers can support school organization with daily check-ins using a simple checklist (homework brought, assignments recorded, materials packed).
