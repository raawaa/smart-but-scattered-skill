# Chapter 5: Ten Principles for Improving Your Child's Executive Skills

## Core Idea
Ten guiding principles — anchored by the ABC model (Antecedent → Behavior → Consequence) — form the foundation for all executive skill interventions, and the three most powerful strategies (environment, teaching, incentives) each get their own chapter.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The ABC Model for Executive Skill Interventions**:
  - **A** (Antecedent): Modify the environment *before* the behavior occurs — Ch 6.
  - **B** (Behavior): Teach the skill directly — Ch 7.
  - **C** (Consequence): Use incentives to motivate performance — Ch 8.
  - When to use: any time you want to change a behavior or build a skill. Use all three for maximum effect.

- **The 10 Principles**:
  1. **Teach deficient skills directly** — don't expect acquisition through observation or osmosis. Use direct instruction: define problem, identify goal, develop instructional sequence with close supervision, then fade supports.
  2. **Consider developmental level** — don't expect a 5-year-old to pack for camp or an 8-year-old to remember asthma medication alone. Use the Developmental Tasks Table (Ch 2) to calibrate.
  3. **Move from external to internal** — always start by changing things outside the child (environment, cues, structure) before requiring the child to change. Example: hold hand at the curb before teaching "look both ways."
  4. **The external includes three levers** — environment (physical/social), task (length, structure, appeal), and interaction style (how you cue, prompt, coach).
  5. **Use the child's drive for mastery and control** — create routines, build in choices, use negotiation, apply "Grandma's Law" (have-to before want-to).
  6. **Modify tasks to match effort capacity** — two types of effortful tasks: (a) not good at it → break down into small steps, (b) don't like doing it → make the first step very easy and immediately reward it. Use the 1–10 Effort Scale.
  7. **Use incentives to augment instruction** — rewards energize effort. Incentives can be simple praise or elaborate point systems. They teach delayed gratification.
  8. **Provide just enough support for success** — two mistakes: too much support (child succeeds but doesn't learn independence) vs. too little support (child fails). Gradually find the sweet spot.
  9. **Keep supports in place until mastery** — 3 weeks is too optimistic for kids with executive skill deficits. Define the problem precisely, measure baseline, and monitor progress. Behaviors may get worse before they get better.
  10. **Fade supports gradually, never abruptly** — like teaching a child to ride a bike: you don't hold on tight then suddenly let go. Let go for seconds, then longer, always catching wobbles.

## Key Concepts
- **Incidental learning vs. direct instruction**: Some children pick up executive skills from loose structure and occasional cues; most need explicit teaching with defined goals and fading support.
- **Grandma's Law**: "Have to" before "want to" — the child must pass through a required task to reach a desired activity. A foundational motivational structure.
- **The 1–10 Effort Scale**: Ask the child to rate a task from 1 (no effort) to 10 (extremely hard). Design the task to feel like a 3. Gradually increase the effort required.
- **Extinction burst**: In early stages of behavior change (especially with emotional control or response inhibition), the problem behavior may increase in duration or intensity before it declines.

## Mental Models
- **You are the external frontal lobes**: Principle 3 — start by doing the executive work for the child (environmental structure) and only gradually transfer it to the child through teaching.
- **Bike-riding as skill acquisition**: You hold, then let go for seconds, then longer. Same for any executive skill — scaffold, test independence, catch wobbles, extend.
- **Effort is a 3, not a 10**: If a task feels like a 10, the child won't start. Design it to feel like a 3 by reducing scope, adding reward, or breaking it down.
- **Get worse before better**: If an intervention involves extinction (ignoring a behavior to reduce it), expect an increase first. Don't abandon the plan — ride out the extinction burst.

## Anti-patterns
- **Teaching by osmosis**: Assuming the child will "pick up" organization by watching you or from occasional reminders. Most children need structured instruction with fading support.
- **Too much support, too long**: Doing the task for the child because it's faster — the child never learns independence.
- **Too little support, too soon**: Removing training wheels and expecting the child to ride perfectly. Middle school demands on executive skills increase dramatically — provide more support, not less.
- **Abrupt withdrawal**: Going from full scaffolding to zero overnight (e.g., suddenly expecting a child to manage their own schedule). Fade gradually.
- **Assuming mastery at 3 weeks**: Children with executive skill deficits often need much longer to internalize a routine. Monitor progress at monthly intervals, not weekly.

## Key Takeaways
1. The ABC model (Antecedent → Behavior → Consequence) gives you three levers for any behavior change. Use all three.
2. Principle 3 (External to Internal) is the golden rule — always modify the environment first before requiring the child to change.
3. Use the 1–10 Effort Scale to calibrate tasks so they feel like a 3 to your child.
4. Incentives are not bribery — they energize effort and build the habit of delayed gratification.
5. "Just enough support" means the child is successful but doing most of the work. You fill in the gaps, not the other way around.
6. Define the problem behavior precisely (e.g., "forgets to hand in homework" not "lazy") and measure baseline before intervening.
7. Expect extinction bursts — the behavior may get worse before it gets better. Stick with the plan.
8. Fade support gradually. Going from all to nothing guarantees failure.
