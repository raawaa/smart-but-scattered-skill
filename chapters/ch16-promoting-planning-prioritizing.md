# Chapter 16: Promoting Planning and Prioritizing

## Core Idea
Planning/prioritization is the ability to create a roadmap to reach a goal (breaking work into sequenced steps with deadlines) and to decide what is most important to focus on — it develops from adult-provided plans in early childhood to self-directed planning by high school, and can be taught through checklist modeling, difficulty-scaling, and incentives.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Long-Term Project Deconstruction (Max's Protocol)**: A method for helping children who are overwhelmed by multi-step assignments.
  - When to use: Child panics, avoids, or completely fails long-term projects despite being capable of daily homework.
  - How: (a) List all steps needed to complete the project; (b) Rate each step on a 1–10 difficulty scale; (c) Revise any step rated >3 to make it simpler; (d) Assign a deadline for each step; (e) Offer bonus points for completing steps early; (f) Points convert to a desired reward (e.g., money toward a video game); (g) Parent provides prompting and check-ins for each step.

- **Planning-Through-Questions Method (Alise's Protocol)**: A guided discovery approach for teaching social planning.
  - When to use: Child wants to arrange social activities but doesn't think ahead far enough.
  - How: (a) Walk the child through a series of planning questions ("Suppose you wanted Jaime to come over. What's the first thing to do?"); (b) Let the child supply the answer at each step; (c) Write the resulting sequence down as a checklist; (d) Parent cues the child earlier in the week to start the process; (e) Gradually fade cueing as the child internalizes the sequence.

- **"Let's Make a Plan" Checklist Method**: A universal planning habit for parents to use with children.
  - When to use: Any time a multi-step task or goal arises — from cleaning a room to preparing for a trip.
  - How: (a) Say "Let's make a plan"; (b) Write steps as a checklist; (c) Child checks each step off as completed; (d) Gradually shift ownership — ask "What do you need to do first? And then what?" — and write the child's dictated steps.

## Key Concepts
- **Planning**: The ability to create a roadmap to reach a goal or complete a task, involving breaking work into sequenced steps with timelines and intermediate deadlines.
- **Prioritization**: The ability to make decisions about what is most important to focus on, and to order tasks accordingly.
- **Home-School Collaboration for Planning**: When a parent shares the planning weakness, teachers can provide the project rubric and interim deadlines; the parent focuses on prompting and celebration.
- **Recurring Activities as Planning Practice**: Structured weekend activities (sports, drama, dance) indirectly teach planning because they require scheduling and prioritizing around fixed times.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "difficulty thermostat" model**: for a child who freezes on complex tasks, rate each step on a 1–10 scale and dial every step down to ≤3 before letting the child tackle it — this keeps the task in the "doable" zone.
- **Think of planning as "reverse engineering from the deadline"**: start with the due date and work backward, assigning each subtask a deadline, so the child sees the project as a connected sequence rather than a looming monolith.
- **Use the "social director" framing**: giving a child ownership of planning social events (playdates, outings) is inherently motivating because the reward is built in — they get to do something fun with friends.
- **Think of a list as a "memory prosthetic"**: the child doesn't need to hold the entire plan in working memory; the checklist does that for them, freeing cognitive resources for execution.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leaving planning to natural consequences**: assuming a child will gradually figure out planning from failure alone is ineffective — the child becomes discouraged and feels incompetent without a taught system.
- **Overscheduling without teaching prioritization**: parents who fill every moment with activities never let the child practice deciding what matters most; the child grows up relying on external scheduling.
- **Underscheduling under a "kids should be kids" philosophy**: giving children no structure at all leaves them to fritter time away on TV/games without learning to plan.
- **Doing the planning FOR the child**: even when the parent is good at planning, the goal is to gradually transfer the skill to the child — always involving them in the "what first, what next" conversation.
- **Ignoring the teacher's help when you share the planning weakness**: parents who can't plan well themselves must enlist teachers to provide the structure and timelines.

## Key Takeaways
1. Say "Let's make a plan" and write it as a checklist — start with adult-led, transition to child-dictated.
2. For long-term projects, break into steps rated on a 1–10 difficulty scale; revise any step over 3.
3. Assign interim deadlines for each project step; offer bonus points for early completion.
4. Use the child's interests (building a tree house, planning a playdate, buying a video game) as naturally motivating contexts for teaching planning.
5. Withhold preferred activities until priority tasks are done ("You can watch TV when your homework is finished").
6. For social planning, walk the child through discovery questions and write their answers into a reusable checklist.
7. When parents share the weakness, teachers must provide the project breakdown and timeline; the parent handles prompting and reward.
