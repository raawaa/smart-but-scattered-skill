# Chapter 22: When What You Do Is Not Enough

## Core Idea
When home interventions fail, systematically audit the plan against seven diagnostic questions covering problem specificity, realistic standards, environmental supports, the skill taught, the teaching procedure, incentives, and consistency—and if the plan is sound but the child's challenges are severe, seek professional help from a behavioral or cognitive-behavioral clinician.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Intervention Diagnostic Audit**: Seven questions to ask when a home plan is not working.
  - When to use: Any time an intervention has been running 14-21 days without meaningful improvement
  - How:
    1. **Problem specificity**: Is the behavior defined precisely enough to judge success/failure? Replace "always/never/everything" with specific what/when/where descriptions.
    2. **Realistic standard**: What specific improvement can you live with? Start with small improvements ("voices displeasure but accepts change without tantrum" rather than "never gets upset").
    3. **Age-appropriate expectations**: Are your expectations realistic for this child's age and current skill level? If you're saying "when I was her age..." you may be reaching too high.
    4. **Environmental supports**: Have you put visual cues, storage systems, labels, or schedules in place?
    5. **Specific skill being taught**: Is the behavior you're trying to teach clearly identified?
    6. **Teaching procedure**: Who teaches, what is the procedure, how often is it practiced? The burden is on the teacher, not the child, especially at the beginning.
    7. **Incentives**: Are you using rewards the child actually values? This is the most frequently missing element.

## Key Concepts
- **One-Trial Learning Fallacy**: The mistaken belief that children should learn important behaviors after a single explanation or experience; in reality, most executive skills require practice over a long period.
- **Can't vs. Won't**: The authors assert they have met very few lazy children—most who appear lazy are discouraged, doubt their abilities, feel it's more punishing to try and fail than not try, or prefer fun to tedious work. The question is not can't vs. won't but what it takes to overcome the obstacle.
- **Antecedents and Consequences**: Behavioral clinicians identify environmental triggers (antecedents) and responses (consequences) that maintain problem behavior, then help parents alter one or both.

## Mental Models
- **Use the "14-21 day rule"** when evaluating a plan's effectiveness: parents typically try a plan for 4-5 days and then become inconsistent—either because they don't see change (so they give up) or because they see immediate improvement (so they slack off). Stick with it for the full trial period.
- **Use the "keep yourself honest" rating** : rate daily how well you stuck to the plan on a 1-5 scale (1 = "I really screwed up today" to 5 = "I stuck to the plan 100%").
- **Think of incentives not as bribes but as signalers**: Rewards signal success to the child. Once the skill is learned, natural incentives (praise, approval, intrinsic satisfaction) can replace external rewards.

## Anti-patterns
- **Intermittent plan adherence**: If the plan is followed only sporadically, the child sees it's not important to you and returns to old behaviors. Plans must be simple enough to fit within the time frames you reliably have available.
- **Inconsistent adult implementation**: If another parent, sibling, or teacher is involved but doesn't follow the key elements, the plan will fail. Get all adults on board before starting.
- **Giving up at 4-5 days**: Parents quit either because they see no change or because they see change and assume the problem is solved. Both lead to relapse within weeks.
- **Using traditional talk therapy or relationship therapy**: For executive skill weaknesses, behavioral or cognitive-behavioral approaches are far more effective than insight-oriented therapies.

## Key Takeaways
1. Before seeking outside help, audit your plan against all seven diagnostic questions—the problem is often in the plan's specificity, the incentives, or the consistency.
2. Be very precise about the problem: what, when, and where, not "always" or "everything." Define the standard you can live with in concrete terms.
3. Build in rewards the child actually values and does not have free access to—this is the most commonly missing element.
4. Stick with a reasonable plan for 14-21 days; use a daily self-rating to keep yourself honest.
5. If you need professional help, seek a clinician using behavioral or cognitive-behavioral approaches with experience in parent training—not talk therapy.
6. Testing may be warranted when: the child needs documentation for school support, there may be additional learning problems (LD, ADHD), or other psychological disorders (anxiety, depression, OCD) may be present.
7. Medication is best considered when the attention disorder affects friendships, self-esteem, learning capacity, or the child's overall emotional adjustment, and should be combined with behavioral interventions rather than used alone.
