# Patterns

## ABC Intervention
**When to use**: Any time you want to change a behavior or build an executive skill. Use all three levers for maximum effect.
**How**: (A) Modify the environment *before* the behavior — use visual cues, schedules, remove distractions. (B) Teach the skill *directly* — define the problem, set a goal, outline steps, create a checklist, supervise, fade. (C) Use *incentives* to motivate performance — praise, points, reward menus, behavior contracts.
**Trade-offs**: Relying on only one lever is rarely sufficient. Environmental modification alone doesn't build skills; teaching without motivation produces resistance; incentives without teaching leave the child not knowing *how* to perform.

## The Game Plan (Intervention Hierarchy)
**When to use**: Starting any intervention with a child; choosing the appropriate level of support.
**How**: (1) Try the minimum — environmental modification, verbal scaffolding, or praise alone. (2) Apply principles from Ch 5. (3) Use ready-made daily routine plans (Ch 10). (4) Target specific executive skill weaknesses (Ch 11–21). Escalate only when the current level is insufficient.
**Trade-offs**: Starting too high (multicomponent plans) wastes effort and can overwhelm the child. Starting too low and persisting without progress delays improvement. Measure outcomes to know when to escalate.

## 6-Step Direct Teaching Sequence
**When to use**: Teaching any new executive skill or behavior systematically.
**How**: (1) Identify the problem behavior in specific, observable terms. (2) Set a positive goal with baseline measurement. (3) Outline the steps the child needs to follow. (4) Turn steps into a checklist or short set of rules. (5) Supervise the child following the procedure with practice sessions. (6) Fade supervision gradually.
**Trade-offs**: Requires time and consistency upfront, but builds lasting independence. Skipping steps (especially practice sessions or baseline measurement) often causes plan failure.

## 4-Stage Fading of Supervision
**When to use**: After a teaching procedure is in place and the child shows emerging competence.
**How**: Stage 1 — Parent directs (tells child what to do step-by-step). Stage 2 — Parent uses visual cues ("Look at your list"). Stage 3 — Parent asks prompting questions ("What do you need to do?"). Stage 4 — Child internalizes and self-directs.
**Trade-offs**: Fading too early causes relapse; fading too late breeds dependence. Each stage may take weeks or months. Err on the side of more time in each stage.

## Formal Incentive System Design
**When to use**: Simple praise is insufficient to motivate the child to practice a skill or complete a routine.
**How**: (1) Describe the problem behavior and set a specific, observable goal. (2) Build a reward menu with daily, weekly, and long-term options (points-based). (3) Write a behavior contract that all parties sign. (4) Evaluate and tinker — expect to make adjustments.
**Trade-offs**: Highly effective when rewards are genuinely valued and not freely accessible. Risk of sibling resentment (explain the system to other children). Parents must follow through consistently or the system collapses.

## Effective Praise Framework
**When to use**: Whenever the child displays a target behavior; aim for a 3:1 ratio of positive to corrective statements.
**How**: Deliver praise (1) immediately after the behavior, (2) specifying the particulars of the accomplishment, (3) providing information about its value, (4) acknowledging effort, and (5) orienting toward self-appreciation and problem-solving.
**Trade-offs**: Requires deliberate attention — it's easy to default to global praise ("Good job!"). More effortful than vague praise but dramatically more effective in building internal motivation.

## Room Cleaning Protocol
**When to use**: Child cannot clean their room independently; executive skill demands include Task Initiation, Sustained Attention, Planning/Prioritization, and Organization.
**How**: (1) Create a step list (clothes → toys → books → desk → trash → return items). (2) Photograph a "clean room" as the objective standard. (3) Create a written or picture checklist for ages 7–10. (4) Supervise throughout initially; fade to end-of-task check. (5) Use backward chaining for high-effort tasks.
**Trade-offs**: Addresses only *one* skill at a time — break down which specific skill the child fails at (starting? sticking with it? knowing where things go?). Works best when combined with a visible reward.

## Morning Routine Protocol
**When to use**: Child is slow getting ready, loses time awareness, or needs repeated prompting (ages 7–10).
**How**: (1) Child creates a picture/written schedule of morning tasks in preferred order. (2) Use Velcro strips so pictures can be rearranged and moved to a "done" pocket. (3) Time the child's baseline over 2 mornings. (4) Set a timer for the total; parent checks in twice. (5) Reward if finished 15+ minutes early.
**Trade-offs**: Child ownership of task order increases buy-in but may cause conflict if the order is inefficient. The timer must be visible (Time Timer). Requires parent to resist the urge to nag.

## Homework Management Protocol
**When to use**: Child avoids, delays, or rushes through homework; parent-child conflict over homework is high (ages 7–14).
**How**: (1) Create a homework plan form — write assignments, check materials, estimate time, set start times. (2) Parent reviews and adjusts. (3) Use visual timer for work blocks with structured breaks. (4) Set a hard finish deadline; child chooses start time based on their own time estimates. (5) If estimates are inaccurate, earlier start time next day. (6) For older children, replace parent with a third-party coach (teacher, counselor) who meets daily for 10 minutes.
**Trade-offs**: Effective at removing parent from the nagging role. Requires teacher coordination for coach-based models. Time estimation feedback loop builds skill but may initially cause frustration.

## Self-Monitoring Audiotape Protocol
**When to use**: Child loses track of time or wanders off-task without noticing.
**How**: Play an audiotape (or app) emitting random tones. When the tone sounds, the child asks "Was I paying attention?" and refocuses if needed. Gradually increase the interval between tones.
**Trade-offs**: Builds self-awareness without parent nagging. Requires the child to cooperate with the monitoring process. Must be paired with environmental supports (timer, distraction removal).

## Long-Term Project Deconstruction (Max's Protocol)
**When to use**: Child panics, avoids, or completely fails multi-step projects despite being capable of daily homework (ages 8–14).
**How**: (1) List all steps needed. (2) Rate each step on a 1–10 difficulty scale; revise any step rated >3. (3) Assign a deadline for each step. (4) Offer bonus points for early completion. (5) Points convert to a desired reward. (6) Parent provides prompting and check-ins per step.
**Trade-offs**: Requires upfront planning from the parent. Closing the difficulty gap (steps at ≤3) is essential — a single overwhelming step can stop the whole project. Works best when the reward is under the parent's control.

## Time Estimation Feedback Loop (Nathan's Protocol)
**When to use**: Child chronically underestimates task duration, leading to rushed or incomplete work.
**How**: (1) Child lists each assignment and estimates time required. (2) Records on a spreadsheet and emails parent. (3) Child decides start time based on estimates with a hard finish deadline (e.g., 9 PM). (4) If any estimate is off by >20 min, child must start earlier next day. (5) If accurate, child chooses start time. (6) Parents check completed work at the stated finish time.
**Trade-offs**: Builds time estimation through immediate, concrete feedback. Requires the child to accurately report estimates. Consequence (earlier start) must feel meaningful but not punitive.

## Start-Time Timer Protocol (Jack's Protocol)
**When to use**: Child understands a chore but requires repeated nagging to begin.
**How**: (1) Child selects a start-time delay (e.g., 5 min). (2) Child uses a visual timer; responds when it signals. (3) Parent cues only to set the timer. (4) If not started within 2 min of the timer, child stops all other activities until chore is done. (5) Free pass (skip one chore) for every 5 days of on-time starts.
**Trade-offs**: Timer replaces the parent as the authority signal, reducing conflict. Requires the child to have a watch or timer they can operate. The free pass incentive works well for maintaining streaks.

## External Coach Model (Colby's Protocol)
**When to use**: Parent-child conflict over homework has become counterproductive, and parental "nagging" triggers resistance in an older child.
**How**: (1) Child selects a trusted coach (teacher, counselor, administrator). (2) Coach meets daily (~10 min) to plan the evening's work. (3) Coach checks in 2–3 times/week by IM or phone. (4) Teachers email weekly feedback to coach. (5) Parents step back from the homework role entirely.
**Trade-offs**: Highly effective when parent-child homework dynamics are adversarial. Requires a willing coach at school; not all schools have staffing. Blind coaching (no teacher feedback) is ineffective. Parents must genuinely step back.

## Family Cleanup Accountability System (Rose Family Protocol)
**When to use**: Multiple children leave belongings strewn around common areas.
**How**: (1) A jar holds $25 in quarters each week. (2) All out-of-place belongings must be picked up by 5:00 PM daily. (3) Each item left out costs one quarter. (4) Impounded items stay in a box for 24 hours. (5) Remaining money is spent on a group activity. (6) Alarm clock (4:30 PM) cues the start.
**Trade-offs**: Group reward creates peer accountability. Requires consistent daily enforcement. Risk of children framing each other's items as "out of place" — define ground rules. The "impound" rule teaches natural consequences without direct punishment.
