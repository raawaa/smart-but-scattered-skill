# Glossary

**504 Plan** — A civil-rights-based accommodation plan ensuring children with disabilities have equal access to education; modifies environment but does not require specialized instruction. (Ch 23)

**ABC Model (Antecedent–Behavior–Consequence)** — The foundational framework for all executive skill interventions: modify the environment (A), teach the skill directly (B), or use incentives (C). (Ch 5)

**Advance Organizer** — A progressive intervention hierarchy: start with the minimum (environment, scaffolding, incentives), then ready-made plans, then targeted skill work. (Ch 9)

**Antecedent** — Environmental conditions or cues that occur *before* a behavior; modifying antecedents is the first intervention lever (the "A" in ABC). (Ch 5, 6)

**Backward Chaining** — Starting the child at the last step of a task and gradually working backward, so the child experiences immediate success at the finish line. (Ch 7, 10)

**Baseline Measurement** — Counting or timing current behavior before intervention to set realistic goals and track progress. (Ch 7)

**Before-During-After Interaction Framework** — Three-phase interaction model: rehearse and cue *before*, coach and monitor *during*, praise and debrief *after*. (Ch 6)

**Behavior** — The target skill or action being taught (the "B" in ABC); addressed through direct teaching and practice. (Ch 5, 7)

**Behavior Contract** — A written agreement defining the child's responsibilities, parents' supports, points/rewards, and consequences. (Ch 8)

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — Therapeutic approach recommended for persistent emotional control problems; uses self-talk, relaxation, and cognitive restructuring. (Ch 13)

**Consequence** — Incentives or outcomes that follow a behavior to motivate performance (the "C" in ABC). (Ch 5, 8)

**Differential Attention** — Attending to on-task/praised behavior while ignoring brief lapses, shifting the incentive toward desired behavior rather than inadvertently reinforcing distraction. (Ch 14)

**Emotional Control** — The ability to manage emotions (temper, anxiety, frustration, disappointment) to sustain goal-directed behavior and access positive emotions during difficulty. (Ch 1, 13)

**Executive Skills** — Brain-based cognitive processes required to plan, direct, and regulate behavior to achieve goals; the 11-skill model (Dawson & Guare) divides them into Thinking skills (Working Memory, Planning/Prioritization, Organization, Time Management, Metacognition) and Doing skills (Response Inhibition, Emotional Control, Sustained Attention, Task Initiation, Goal-Directed Persistence, Flexibility). (Ch 1)

**Executive Skills Questionnaires** — Age-appropriate rating scales (Preschool–Middle School) to assess each of the 11 executive skills using 33 items (3 per skill). (Ch 2)

**Extinction Burst** — The temporary increase in problem behavior that often occurs in early stages of behavior change before it declines. (Ch 5)

**Fading** — Gradually reducing support/supervision in defined stages (direct → visual cues → prompting questions → self-direction); never abrupt. (Ch 5, 7)

**Flexibility** — The ability to revise plans in the face of obstacles, setbacks, new information, or mistakes. (Ch 1, 19)

**Goal-Directed Persistence** — Setting a goal and sustaining effort toward it over time despite competing interests, frustration, or lack of immediate payoff. (Ch 1, 20)

**Goodness of Fit** — The degree of match between a task's executive skill demands and the child's current skill set, or between parent and child profiles. (Ch 3, 4)

**Grandma's Law (First/Then)** — Less preferred tasks must precede more preferred tasks; foundational motivational structure. (Ch 5, 8)

**Home–School Report Card** — A coordination tool where teachers communicate points earned at school and rewards are delivered at home. (Ch 8)

**IEP (Individualized Education Program)** — A formal special education plan with measurable annual goals for executive skills when a child qualifies for specialized instruction. (Ch 23)

**Intervention Diagnostic Audit** — Seven diagnostic questions to ask when a plan isn't working: problem specificity, realistic standard, age-appropriateness, environmental supports, specific skill taught, teaching procedure, incentives. (Ch 22)

**Jump-Start Strategy** — Timing a persistence or savings plan to coincide with a windfall (birthday money, holiday gifts) so the child sees rapid initial progress. (Ch 20)

**Metacognition** — The ability to take a bird's-eye view of oneself, self-monitor, self-evaluate, and adjust based on evaluation; spans task performance (checking work against a standard) and social situations (reading others' reactions). (Ch 1, 21)

**Natural Consequences** — Consequences that flow naturally from behavior (e.g., catching up on homework over the weekend); recommended for teens over arbitrary punishments. (Ch 8, 24)

**One-Trial Learning Fallacy** — The mistaken belief that children should learn important behaviors after a single explanation; most executive skills require prolonged practice. (Ch 22)

**Organization** — The ability to establish and maintain a system for arranging or keeping track of important items. (Ch 1, 17)

**Planning/Prioritization** — The ability to create a roadmap (sequenced steps with timelines) to reach a goal and decide what is most important. (Ch 1, 16)

**Premack Principle** — See Grandma's Law: using a high-probability behavior (preferred activity) to reinforce a low-probability behavior (less preferred task). (Ch 5)

**Response Inhibition** — The capacity to think before acting, resist the urge to say or do something before evaluating the situation; the foundational executive skill. (Ch 1, 11)

**RTI (Response to Intervention)** — A tiered assessment-intervention approach beginning with high-quality classroom instruction, then adding increasingly intensive interventions as needed without requiring a formal disability label. (Ch 23)

**Scaffolding** — Providing just enough support for the child to be successful, emphasizing connections between concepts, patterns, and prior knowledge. (Ch 7)

**Scattered** — A child who has intelligence but cannot consistently organize output — deciding what to do when and controlling behavior to get there. (Ch 0, 1)

**Self-Questioning Routine** — A four-question internal script: (1) What is the problem? (2) What is my plan? (3) Am I following my plan? (4) How did I do? (Ch 21)

**Social Stories** — Brief vignettes with descriptive, perspective, and directive sentence types that help children understand social information and handle interactions. (Ch 19)

**Special Education** — Requires a documented educational disability that adversely affects performance; can address organization, time management, and planning through direct teaching. (Ch 23)

**Sustained Attention** — The capacity to keep paying attention despite distractions, fatigue, or boredom; distinct from interest-driven focus. (Ch 1, 14)

**Task Initiation** — The ability to begin projects or activities without undue procrastination, in an efficient or timely manner — specifically for tasks the child finds unpleasant. (Ch 1, 15)

**Think-Aloud Procedure** — Parent models the internal thought process for tackling open-ended tasks aloud, so the child can internalize the reasoning structure. (Ch 10)

**Time Management** — The capacity to estimate how much time one has, how to allocate it, and how to stay within time limits and deadlines. (Ch 1, 18)

**Token Economy** — A formal incentive system where points or tokens are earned for target behaviors and exchanged for rewards from a menu. (Ch 8)

**Verbal Scaffolding** — Using questions (not commands), explanations (not dictation), and encouragement of self-appraisal in everyday interactions to build understanding and metacognition. (Ch 7)

**Visual Timer (Time Timer)** — A clock with a decreasing red disk showing remaining time visually; essential for children who struggle with abstract time concepts. (Ch 14, 18)

**Weak Organ Theory** — Under stress, the weakest executive skills deteriorate first — like the weakest organ in an illness. (Ch 3)

**Working Memory** — The capacity to hold information in mind while performing complex tasks; applying past learning to present situations. (Ch 1, 12)
